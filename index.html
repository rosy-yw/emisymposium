About the Symposium

Join us for the CUHK–UCL English-medium instruction symposium, held on 24 April 2026 at the Department of Curriculum and Instruction, The Chinese University of Hong Kong. Under the theme English-medium instruction in the era of GenAI: Negotiating tradition and modernity, the symposium brings together leading voices from Hong Kong and the international research community to examine how EMI is being transformed by generative AI across higher education. The event addresses questions of human agency, participation, authorship, assessment, academic language, teacher development, research practice, and educational inequality in a rapidly changing multilingual landscape.

Keynote speakers

Glenn Stockwell

The Education University of Hong Kong

Bio

Professor Glenn Stockwell is a leading scholar in applied linguistics and educational technology, specialising in computer-assisted language learning. He previously served as Professor of Applied Linguistics at Waseda University and is Editor-in-Chief of Computer Assisted Language Learning.

Talk title

The Collaboration Illusion: AI and Human Agency in EMI

Abstract

This keynote questions the now common description of human–AI interaction as collaboration in EMI. It argues that AI lacks the intentionality, reciprocity, and accountability required for genuine collaboration, and calls for more precise ways of describing AI’s pedagogical role.

Jim McKinley

University College London

Bio

Prof. Jim McKinley researches implications of globalization for L2 writing, language education, and higher education studies, especially the teaching-research nexus and English medium instruction. He is Editor-in-Chief of System and incoming Editor of TESOL Quarterly.

Talk title

Authorship, participation, and academic voice in English-medium instruction in the age of generative AI

Abstract

This talk argues that generative AI challenges basic assumptions about participation, authorship, and linguistic competence in EMI. Rather than treating AI as either threat or solution, it asks how EMI pedagogy might reconceptualise participation when academic work increasingly becomes a hybrid human–AI practice.

The Hong Kong Polytechnic University

Bio

Professor Christy Xuyan Qiu researches second language teaching and learning, especially academic genre knowledge, task-based pedagogy, and how English language education prepares students for EMI in Chinese contexts.

Talk title

Human vs. AI Interaction: EMI University Students’ Discourse Marker Choices in Oral Tasks

Abstract

This study examines how task complexity, pragmatic demand, and interaction modality shape EMI students’ discourse marker use in oral tasks with both human interlocutors and ChatGPT. The findings suggest that task design and AI-mediated interaction both influence how spoken communication develops in L2 contexts.

Bo Peng

The University of Hong Kong

Bio

Bo Peng is a PhD candidate at the Faculty of Education, The University of Hong Kong. Her work focuses on EMI, teacher professional development, researcher-practitioner collaboration, and the role of AI in supporting teacher development.

Talk title

Developing EMI teacher language awareness through AI-teacher collaboration

Abstract

This design-based study examines how a novice EMI accounting teacher’s language awareness developed over one semester through collaboration with AI. The findings show increased attention to students’ language-related challenges, stronger scaffolding practices, and more language-sensitive assessment design.

Bio

Professor Gabriel Fan Fang works across sociolinguistics, language education, Global Englishes, translanguaging, EMI, and teacher education. His research addresses accent, identity, native-speaker ideology, and equitable multilingual pedagogies.

Talk title

English medium instruction: Preparedness and Support in the era of GenAI

Abstract

This talk reports two studies from the Chinese context and argues that EMI must be examined beyond the classroom. It highlights insufficient student preparedness, concerns about teaching quality, and the risk that AI-mediated EMI practices may reproduce existing inequalities unless designed with stronger language support and equity in mind.

Talk title

Measuring academic language proficiency for EMI studies: Development of an academic c-test with GenAI

Abstract

This paper presents the development of an academic c-test supported by GenAI as an efficient way of assessing students’ academic English proficiency in EMI contexts. It introduces the validity of c-tests, explains the development process, and considers both research and pedagogical implications.

Benjamin Luke Moorhouse

City University of Hong Kong

Bio

Prof. Benjamin Luke Moorhouse is Associate Professor in the Department of English at City University of Hong Kong. His work focuses on teachers’ lived experiences, competencies, and professional learning, and he is currently exploring the impact of GenAI on language teaching and learning.

Talk title

The case against AI-mediated scholarship in EMI

Abstract

This talk critiques the growing dependence on AI-mediated scholarship in EMI, especially summariser and chat-with-the-article tools. It argues instead for scaffolded practices of authentic engagement, such as slow reading, annotation, discussion, and genre-aware routines, in order to preserve intellectual struggle as central to learning.

Talk title

Narrating the lived experience of EMI: Participant Narratives as a proposal to advance interpretive transparency in the age of GenAI and open science

Abstract

This talk proposes Participant Narratives as a way of improving interpretive transparency in qualitative EMI research. Rather than yielding to simplistic versions of openness, it argues for a form of rigor that gives fuller context, sustains ethical responsibility, and remains faithful to the ethos of qualitative inquiry.
